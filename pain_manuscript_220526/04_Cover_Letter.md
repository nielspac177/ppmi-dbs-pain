Editor-in-Chief
*JAMA Neurology*

Dear Editor,

Please consider for publication in *JAMA Neurology* our manuscript entitled "**Deep brain stimulation, pain trajectory, and the symptom architecture of Parkinson disease: a target-trial-emulation matched longitudinal cohort in the Parkinson's Progression Markers Initiative.**"

Pain affects 40 to 85 % of patients with Parkinson disease and is among the most burdensome non-motor symptoms. Deep brain stimulation (DBS) is an established motor therapy with an emerging non-motor literature, but no prior study has applied a matched longitudinal target-trial-emulation framework to test whether DBS alters the long-term pain trajectory in PD. We present that analysis, with a primary inverse-probability-of-censoring-weighted estimator and sixteen pre-registered and post-hoc robustness analyses; the conclusions are concordant across all of them.

The headline result is a non-inferiority statement: the between-arm change in MDS-UPDRS Part I item 9 pain at the 6 to 18-month landmark was −0.053 points (95 % CI −0.293, +0.187), with non-inferiority concluded at every pre-specified margin from ±1 down to ±0.3 MDS-UPDRS-I points (TOST P = .009 at the tightest margin). A duration-matched sequential-trial emulation sensitivity reinforces this conclusion under explicit immortal-time correction.

Beyond the primary safety conclusion, three directional, hypothesis-generating mechanistic signals — a pre-specified late-post Network Comparison Test (uncorrected P = .050), a directionally lower within-patient pain–motor Spearman coupling (matched bootstrap Δρ = −0.16), and a uniformly null six-candidate single-mediator analysis (LEDD, Hoehn &amp; Yahr, GDS, SCOPA, ESS, UPDRS-III; all ACME P ≥ .15) — are jointly compatible with a cautious reframing of DBS toward a "symptom-architecture-modulating" therapy. We are explicit that none of these secondary signals is by itself definitive in this cohort, and we recommend prospective replication.

We believe this work is appropriate for *JAMA Neurology* for three reasons. First, the safety question is clinically actionable: patients counselled for DBS routinely ask whether the procedure will affect their pain over the long term, and the literature to date has been single-arm or short-follow-up. Second, the methodology — target-trial emulation, inverse-probability-of-censoring weighting, a margin grid, sequential-trial emulation, and a multi-analysis robustness layer — is a portable framework that we and others can apply to other non-motor PD endpoints. Third, the entire analysis is openly reproducible: a synthetic PPMI-shaped fixture, Docker container, GitHub Codespaces configuration, and eight Architecture Decision Records mean reviewers and replicators can exercise the pipeline end-to-end without applying to PPMI.

This work has not been published elsewhere and is not under consideration at another journal. All authors have read and approved the manuscript. No conflicts of interest are declared. [Or: revise as appropriate.]
