## Key Points

**Question.** Does deep brain stimulation alter the long-term course of pain in Parkinson disease relative to medical therapy, and does it reshape the multivariate non-motor symptom architecture surrounding pain?

**Findings.** In a target-trial-emulation matched longitudinal cohort of 1,484 PPMI participants, the inverse-probability-of-censoring-weighted between-arm change in MDS-UPDRS Part I item 9 pain at the 6–18-month landmark was −0.053 (95 % CI −0.293, +0.187), with non-inferiority concluded at margins down to ±0.3 points. Directional, hypothesis-generating signals — a pre-specified late-post Network Comparison Test (uncorrected P = .050), a lower within-patient pain–motor coupling, and a uniformly null multi-mediator analysis — are jointly compatible with a cautious reframing of stimulation as a symptom-architecture-modulating therapy.

**Meaning.** Deep brain stimulation does not worsen the 4-year pain trajectory in early Parkinson disease, with hypothesis-generating evidence supporting a wider reshaping of non-motor symptom architecture pending prospective replication.
